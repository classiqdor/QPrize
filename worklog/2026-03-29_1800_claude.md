### Truncated var_len experiment (attempt_005)
- Created `attempts/attempt_005_2026-03-29_truncated_varlen.py`
- Tested 6-bit (n=31) with var_len=4 (N=16) and var_len=3 (N=8)

**Results:**
| var_len | N | CX | Qubits | Result |
|---------|---|----|--------|--------|
| 5 (full) | 32 | 1252 | 16 | ✅ d=18 |
| 4 | 16 | 1022 | 14 | ❌ top=d=0 (signal destroyed) |
| 3 | 8 | 778 | 12 | ❌ top=d=30 (signal destroyed) |

**Key finding**: For Shor's period finding, you need N = 2^var_len ≥ n. With N < n, multiple QFT peaks fold onto the same measurement outcome, destroying the signal. var_len=4 gives d=18 fewer hits than the uniform distribution baseline (~66) — it doesn't even appear in the top 5.

**Lesson**: Truncated var_len is NOT a valid optimization for this algorithm. Full precision is required.

Pattern: CX = 122 - 6 × (trailing zeros of k). Classiq's synthesizer already exploits this.

Total CX from 10 additions: g_steps [1,2,4,8,16] = 550, negq_steps [13,26,21,11,22] = 598 → **1148 CX**
Remaining (QFTs, overhead): **104 CX**
Total: **1252 CX** ✅ matches measurement

**Key insight**: Even if g_steps are powers of 2 (special structure), Classiq already exploits this. The savings from power-of-2 constants are modest (at most 6 CX per trailing zero). The real bottleneck is the modular reduction overhead (~100 CX per adder, vs ~5 CX for mod-2^k arithmetic).

For n=31 = 2^5 - 1 (Mersenne prime), the modular reduction can be simplified:
- Standard: compare and conditional subtract (costly)

If successful: could reduce from ~1100 CX to ~50 CX for all 10 additions. Total: ~150 CX for 6-bit. This would be transformative.
